CONCLUSIONS/ RESULTS:

R1) In reg2 the interaction has a -0.03 reduction in the BMI of the average NYorker.p-value is very high. The calorie labeling policy in New York does not show a statistically significant effect on BMI in this sample. The near-zero estimate and high p-value suggest no meaningful change in BMI in NY after the policy, relative to control states.

R2) Eating fries frequently is strongly and positively associated with BMI. For every unit increase in fryoverload, BMI increases by roughly 0.782.3 units, depending on whether we account for nonlinear effects.

Estimate 0.78 to 2.31 (depending on the model) p-value < 0.001 (highly significant)

R3) In the quadratic model (reg4), fryoverload_sq has: Estimate -0.037 p-value < 0.001 (highly significant)

The negative coefficient suggests diminishing returns: as fryoverload increases, the additional effect on BMI becomes smaller. In simple terms, the BMI rise slows down at very high levels of fry consumption.
